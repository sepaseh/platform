# Problem Discovery

> Before designing a solution, make sure you're solving the right problem.

---

## Why This Matters

Many software projects fail long before implementation begins.

Not because the code is poor.

Not because the architecture is wrong.

But because the team starts solving a problem they never truly understood.

Problem Discovery is the engineering process of understanding **why a project should exist** before deciding **how it should be built**.

---

## Start With "Why"

Avoid starting with questions like:

- What features do you need?
- What screens should the system have?
- Which technology should we use?

Instead, ask:

> **Why is the current way of working no longer good enough?**

If today's process already works, there must be a reason someone is investing time and money to change it.

Finding that reason is the first responsibility of a software engineer.

---

## Understand the Business Before the Software

Software engineers solve business problems.

To do that, they must first understand how the business actually works.

The most reliable source of that understanding depends on the project.

It may come from:

- observing the real workflow,
- working alongside users,
- a trusted domain expert,
- product specialists,
- or validated user behavior and data.

The goal is always the same:

> Build an accurate understanding of the business domain before proposing solutions.

Observation is only one technique—not the objective itself.

---

## Understand the Workflow Before Judging It

Understanding a workflow does **not** mean accepting it.

Study the current process to understand:

- why it exists,
- which constraints shaped it,
- where the bottlenecks are,
- and which business scenarios it must support.

Only after understanding it should you ask:

> Should this workflow be preserved, improved, or replaced?

Never redesign a process you haven't fully understood.

---

## Look for Patterns, Not Products

Once the problem becomes clear, experience begins to help.

Experienced engineers rarely remember specific solutions.

Instead, they recognize familiar problem patterns.

A solution from a completely different industry may become relevant because the underlying behavior is similar—not because the products are related.

Do not search for similar software.

Search for similar problem-solving patterns.

---

## Research Comes After Understanding

A common mistake is researching technologies before understanding the problem.

A better sequence is:

1. Discover why the project exists.
2. Understand the business domain.
3. Understand the current workflow.
4. Challenge the workflow with real business scenarios.
5. Research possible approaches.
6. Design the solution.

Research should validate an understanding—not replace it.

---

## When There Is No Existing Workflow

Some products solve problems that have no existing software or established workflow.

In these cases, certainty cannot be obtained through observation.

Instead:

- build the smallest valuable solution,
- release it quickly,
- learn from real users,
- and let the market validate or reject the assumptions.

The goal remains unchanged:

Reduce uncertainty before making large investments.

Only the learning method changes.

---

## Engineering Principles

- Discover the business problem before discussing the software.
- Optimize for domain understanding, not for interviews or observations.
- Understand workflows before deciding whether to improve them.
- Challenge workflows with real business scenarios—not personal opinions.
- Research after understanding the problem.
- Recognize problem patterns instead of copying existing products.
- When no workflow exists, learn through rapid validation with the market.

---

## Final Thought

Software engineering begins long before architecture diagrams, APIs, databases, or user interfaces.

The first responsibility of an engineer is not to design a solution.

It is to discover the real problem worth solving.
