# Before You Commit

**Status:** Draft
**Version:** 0.1

> Every commitment consumes something valuable.
>
> Before committing to a project, pause for a moment.
> This document is not here to make decisions for you.
> It exists to help you ask better questions before making one.

---

# Why This Document Exists

Every software project begins with a commitment.

A commitment to a client.

A commitment to a team.

A commitment to future maintenance.

A commitment to spending time, money, trust, and attention.

Once that commitment is made, changing direction becomes increasingly expensive.

This document is not intended to help you reject projects.

It exists to help you avoid committing to the wrong solution before understanding the real problem.

Take a moment.

Think before you commit.

---

# Questions Before You Commit

## 1. What problem am I actually solving?

Describe the problem in your own words.

Avoid repeating the client's request.
Instead, explain the problem you believe truly exists.

---

## 2. Am I solving a request or a need?

Clients often describe solutions.

Your responsibility is to understand the need behind them.

---

## 3. Is software the right solution?

If technology placed no limitations on you, would software still be the best way to solve this problem?

Could a process change, documentation, training, or an existing product create a better outcome?

---

## 4. What are the real constraints?

Before designing a solution, identify the constraints.

Examples include:

* Budget
* Timeline
* Team experience
* Infrastructure
* Operational cost
* Maintenance capability
* Business maturity

Understanding constraints is often more valuable than designing clever solutions.

---

## 5. What is the smallest solution that can validate the idea?

Avoid solving tomorrow's problems with today's budget.

Start with the smallest solution capable of validating the core idea.

Complexity should be earned.

---

## 6. What resources am I responsible for protecting?

Software engineers don't only build software.

They become responsible for resources that belong to other people.

Think about:

* Time
* Budget
* Team energy
* Client trust
* Long-term maintenance

Treat those resources with the same care you would expect from someone working on your own project.

---

## 7. If this project fails, why will it fail?

Imagine the project has already failed.

List the most likely reasons.

Thinking about failure early often improves your chances of success.

---

## 8. Would my future self defend today's decision?

Imagine returning to this project five years from now.

Would you still make the same decisions?

If not, what would you change?

---

# Final Reminder

This document is not intended to create hesitation.

It exists to replace impulsive decisions with intentional ones.

You will never have perfect information.

You only need enough understanding to make a responsible commitment.

Build with confidence.

Commit with awareness.
